# Format Guidelines

These rules apply to all content formats. They are about the craft of writing for X, not about brand voice (which is covered separately).

---

## X-Specific Writing Rules

### Line Breaks
Use line breaks intentionally. A single tweet can have line breaks — they create rhythm and make content easier to scan. One idea per line or two short lines per idea. Never a wall of text.

### Thread Construction
- Tweet 1 must stand alone. If someone sees only Tweet 1 in their timeline, it should be worth reading on its own and create enough curiosity to click through.
- Each tweet in a thread must earn its place. Cut any tweet that is just a transition or a summary of what just came before.
- Threads that could be a single tweet are worse as threads. Don't thread because you have "more to say" — thread because the structure of a thread serves the content.
- End each tweet in a thread (except the last) with a forward pull — not a cliffhanger, but a reason to keep reading.

### Sentence Construction
- Vary sentence length. One short sentence. Then a longer one that develops the idea more fully. Then a very short one to land. This rhythm creates pace.
- Active voice always. Passive voice is for academic papers, not X.
- Contractions are fine: "it's", "don't", "I've", "you're". Contractions are human.

### What NOT to Do
- Don't write in paragraph blocks. Break it up.
- Don't use semicolons in posts. Too formal.
- Don't start sentences with "Additionally," "Furthermore," "Moreover," — these are essay transitional phrases, not conversation.
- Don't end single posts with "What do you think?" unless the post actually invites a specific type of response. Generic engagement bait is off-brand.
- Don't write in the third person about "developers" when Dave can say "I" or "you."

---

## Character Count Management

X displays tweets fully up to 280 characters. Beyond that, it cuts off with "Show more." Use this deliberately:

- Single takes: 80–240 characters is the sweet spot. Short enough to read instantly, long enough to say something real.
- Thread openers: Aim for 220–280 — long enough to create substance, short enough to not feel like a wall.
- Thread body tweets: 180–260 — enough to develop one idea fully.

Do not pad to hit a character target. Do not cut to stay under a target if the cut weakens the post.

---

## Punctuation Style

- Em dashes (—) are fine and on-brand. Use them to create pause or add a parenthetical.
- Ellipses (...) should be rare. They can feel passive or uncertain. Use only when trailing off is intentional.
- Exclamation marks: maximum one per post, and only when the content genuinely calls for emphasis. Zero is usually better.
- Colons before lists: fine and clean. "Three things I learned: / 1. X / 2. Y / 3. Z"

---

## Numeric Style

- Use numerals, not words: "3 hours" not "three hours". Numerals are faster to read on a timeline.
- Percentages: use % symbol, not "percent".
- Large numbers: "4k" or "4,000" — both fine. "four thousand" is not.
