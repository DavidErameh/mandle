# Hook Templates

These are the opening patterns available to the generator. Select the most fitting hook type for the extracted point. Do not default to the same hook repeatedly. Match the hook to the emotional register of the content.

---

## Hook Type 1: The Blunt Fact
**Use when:** The extracted point contains a specific, surprising, or counterintuitive piece of information.
**Pattern:** State the surprising fact, cold. No setup. No "did you know."
**Example structure:**
- "[Specific thing] doesn't work the way most devs think."
- "The [X] everyone recommends breaks the moment [specific condition]."
- "[Number] of the [total] devs I've talked to got this wrong."

---

## Hook Type 2: The Specific Failure
**Use when:** The content involves something breaking, a wrong assumption, or a learning from failure.
**Pattern:** Drop into the failure in medias res. Make it concrete.
**Example structure:**
- "Spent [time] on a bug. Turned out [specific, unexpected cause]."
- "The thing I was sure would work: [X]. The thing that actually worked: [Y]."
- "Broke [thing] three ways. The third break taught me everything."

---

## Hook Type 3: The Contrarian Opening
**Use when:** The point challenges a widely-held developer belief or practice.
**Pattern:** State the conventional wisdom, then immediately undercut it.
**Example structure:**
- "Everyone says [common advice]. That advice is written for [who it actually applies to], not you."
- "[Popular practice] is correct. For teams. Solo devs should do the opposite."
- "The hype around [thing] is real. The part they skip is [specific problem]."

---

## Hook Type 4: The Day Series Anchor
**Use when:** Content connects to the build-in-public journey or progress milestones.
**Pattern:** Day X format. Specific goal. Honest status.
**Example structure:**
- "Day [X] of cracking [challenge] to [specific goal]."
- "Day [X]. [Honest one-line status]. Here's what changed."
**Note:** This hook only works when the content is genuinely connected to a build milestone. Do not force it onto external source material.

---

## Hook Type 5: The Curiosity Gap
**Use when:** The content has a reveal — the interesting part is not the setup, it's what you discovered.
**Pattern:** Create tension between what the reader expects and what actually happened.
**Example structure:**
- "I thought [X] was the bottleneck. It wasn't."
- "The [tool/approach] everyone told me to avoid turned out to be [specific outcome]."
- "Asked [source] about [thing]. The answer surprised me."

---

## Hook Type 6: The Direct Opinion
**Use when:** The point is a position Dave can take a clear stance on.
**Pattern:** State the opinion plainly. No hedging. No "in my opinion."
**Example structure:**
- "[Common tool/practice] is overrated. Here's what actually matters."
- "Most [type of content] is written for the wrong person."
- "The [thing] that gets all the attention isn't the thing that matters."

---

## Hook Type 7: The Specific Observation
**Use when:** The content contains a pattern, trend, or recurring behavior worth naming.
**Pattern:** Name the pattern specifically — not in general terms.
**Example structure:**
- "Every time I've seen a [type of project] fail, it failed at [specific point]."
- "The pattern across every [type of dev] who's shipped something real: [specific behavior]."
- "There's a thing that happens when [specific condition]: [specific outcome]."

---

## Hook Type 8: The Before/After
**Use when:** The content describes a change in approach, understanding, or output.
**Pattern:** Contrast two states. Be specific about both.
**Example structure:**
- "Before [event/discovery]: [state]. After: [state]."
- "[X] weeks ago I was doing [old approach]. Here's what I do now and why."
- "The version I shipped vs. the version I'd ship now: [specific difference]."
