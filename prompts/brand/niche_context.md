# Niche Context — @daviderameh\_

## The Window

This is not a "learn to code" account. It is not a reverse engineering account. It is
not a design account or an agency account. It is all of these, seen through one window:

**Product Engineering · Design Engineering · Development**

Every piece of content produced by this system must pass through this window. Content
that doesn't connect to at least one of these three dimensions — even indirectly — does
not belong here.

The window is the translation layer. Raw source material enters from any direction.
What exits is always shaped by this specific lens: how products are built, how design
and engineering intersect in practice, and what the reality of development actually
looks like at the solo and small-team level.

---

## The Three Dimensions

### Product Engineering

How products come together end to end. Not just writing features — the decisions
that determine what gets built, in what order, and why. Architecture choices that
become constraints. Scope decisions that determine whether something ships. The gap
between what a product is supposed to do and what it actually does in the hands of
real users. Technical debt that was a reasonable trade-off until it wasn't.

**What this dimension surfaces in content:**

- The decisions behind how a product is structured, not just what it does
- Where technical constraints shaped product direction (or should have)
- What breaks first, and why that's always the thing nobody planned for
- The real cost of skipping steps that seem optional until they aren't

### Design Engineering

Where design thinking and engineering execution are the same discipline. Not UI polish.
Not "make it look good." The practice of building things where form and function are
treated as inseparable from the start. How the best builders hold both creative judgment
and technical execution simultaneously. Why engineers who ignore design constraints ship
products that are harder to use than they needed to be, and why designers who ignore
implementation constraints produce work that can't survive contact with reality.

**What this dimension surfaces in content:**

- Where a design decision has a direct engineering consequence, or vice versa
- The craft principles that apply equally to code structure and interface decisions
- How intentional shaping — of APIs, of flows, of interactions — changes outcomes
- The "this is why it feels right" reasoning that most technical content skips entirely

### Development

The actual practice. Writing code, debugging systems, shipping software, operating
things in production. Not the aspirational version — the real version. What it's like
to build something alone or in a very small team, with real constraints, real timelines,
and real consequences when things break. The specific, unglamorous, honest account of
how software gets made.

**What this dimension surfaces in content:**

- Specific mechanisms: how something actually works, not just what it does
- Failure as data: what broke, what it taught, what changed as a result
- The solo vs. team gap: when standard advice doesn't apply to someone building alone
- The skipped explanation: the thing a source assumes you know but never says

## Content Pillars

### Pillar 1: Under the Hood

Deconstructing how things actually work. Not "here's a tutorial on X" but "here's
what X is actually doing that its docs don't explain." Systems thinking applied to
real products, tools, and infrastructure. The gap between the public explanation and
the technical reality.

**Extraction priority:** Non-obvious mechanisms, undocumented behavior, architecture
decisions that have consequences the author may not have flagged.

### Pillar 2: Build in Public — Real Time

The SkyNetGo story and any active build Dave is working on. Progress that is honest,
not performed. Milestone posts anchored to real events. The Day X series format.
This pillar is always personal and always grounded in something actually happening.

**Extraction priority:** Source material that connects to ISP infrastructure, emerging
market tech, hardware-adjacent products, bootstrapped builds with real constraints,
or the specific experience of building software products for real users.

### Pillar 3: The Design-Engineering Intersection

Content that most technical accounts ignore: where craft, aesthetics, and intentional
shaping of things intersects with engineering execution. The philosophy of building
well, not just building functionally. Why the best products feel inevitable in
retrospect — because someone made deliberate decisions that most engineers skip.

**Extraction priority:** Any moment in a source where a creative or design decision
has a measurable engineering outcome, or where an engineering constraint shaped a
product or design decision in a non-obvious way.

### Pillar 4: Anti-Hype Analysis

Honest evaluation of tools, frameworks, AI products, and industry narratives. Not
cynicism — critical assessment backed by specific reasoning. What a tool is actually
good for, where it fails, who it does and doesn't serve. The counter-position to
whatever is getting breathless coverage right now.

**Extraction priority:** Any position in source material that challenges the mainstream
narrative about a popular tool, trend, or practice — especially if the argument is
specific rather than vague.

### Pillar 5: The Solo Builder's Reality

Content that maps the gap between how development is described in industry content
and how it actually goes for someone building alone or in a very small team. Most
technical content is written for teams. This pillar exists for the person who doesn't
have a team — and needs advice that actually applies to their situation.

**Extraction priority:** Any insight in source material that is specifically relevant
to solo or small-team builders — or that reveals a gap between standard advice and
the solo-builder experience.

### Pillar 6: Practitioner Truth

The operational reality of building products, running an agency, and getting paid for
technical work. What clients actually want. What separates a developer who ships from
one who doesn't. The honest account of what it costs — in time, in decisions, in
trade-offs — to do this work professionally.

**Extraction priority:** Specific, honest, operational truths from practitioners who
are actually doing the work. Numbers, timelines, admissions, adjustments. The thing
they say that contradicts the polished version of the story.

## The Four Source Lenses

Source material enters this pipeline from four types of sources. Each lens requires
a different extraction posture.

### Lens 1 — Trending Tech

Sources that cover what's new, what changed, what was just released. Fast-moving,
high-energy, often heavy on announcement and light on analysis.

**Extract:** The practical implication that gets buried under the hype. What this
actually changes — or doesn't — for someone building a product alone. The question
nobody is asking about this trend. The constraint this new thing doesn't solve.

**Do not extract:** The announcement itself. The feature list. Anything that reads
as coverage of the trend rather than analysis of it.

### Lens 2 — Design + Creative Craft

Sources that think about software, interfaces, and products through a creative and
philosophical lens. How decisions get made. Why things are shaped the way they are.

**Extract:** Where design thinking changes engineering outcomes. Craft principles that
apply equally to code and to form. The "this is why it works" reasoning. Any moment
where creative judgment and technical execution are treated as the same discipline.

**Do not extract:** Aesthetic opinions without reasoning. Tool recommendations without
analysis. Content that is about design as decoration rather than design as decision-making.

### Lens 3 — Raw Practitioner Honesty

Sources from people who are actually operating — building, shipping, charging for work,
failing, adjusting. No-filter accounts of how things actually go.

**Extract:** The honest admission that contradicts the polished version. The specific
number or timeline that makes a claim real. The "nobody talks about this" moment. The
gap between how building is described and how it actually happens.

**Do not extract:** Motivation content. General productivity advice. Anything that
sounds like it was written for an audience rather than as an honest account.

### Lens 4 — Unfiltered Opinion

Sources with strong positions. Arguments that don't hedge. Takes on what's wrong with
how the industry operates or what people are getting backwards.

**Extract:** The specific argument being made — not just the conclusion, but the
reasoning. The assumption being challenged. The position that would make a technical
audience stop and either strongly agree or strongly disagree.

**Do not extract:** Vague contrarianism without a specific claim. Hot takes that
don't have an argument behind them. Opinions that contradict brand_rules.md.

## Audience

**Primary:** Builders. Solo developers, indie founders, small-team product engineers
who are technical themselves and are building real things. They are 20–38. They are
tired of content that describes building without capturing what building actually feels
like. They follow accounts that tell the truth about how hard it is and how good it
feels when it works.

**Secondary:** Technical founders and startup engineers who manage small teams or are
individual contributors with taste. They follow for the no-BS analysis and the takes
that apply to real companies with real constraints — not theoretical best practices.

**What they are tired of:** Tutorial content that skips the why. AI hype that ignores
the actual product problem. Advice written for FAANG engineers that makes no sense for
a solo builder. Design content that ignores engineering reality. Engineering content
that ignores design. Motivation content that has no specifics. Polished "build in
public" that hides the failures.

**What they want:** The specific thing the source skipped. The honest account of what
it actually took. The angle that connects design decisions to engineering outcomes.
The take that names the thing everyone is thinking but nobody is saying.

## Relevance Scoring Adjustments

When scoring extracted points, apply these modifiers:

**+2** — Challenges a widely-held assumption in the product, design, or dev world
**+2** — Contains a specific, non-obvious mechanism or decision
**+2** — Has a direct translation through the design-engineering intersection
**+1** — Applicable specifically to solo or small-team builders
**+1** — Has a "here's what they don't tell you" angle
**+1** — Connects to the SkyNetGo story or emerging market tech

**-3** — Basic tutorial-level content with no angle
**-2** — Written primarily for large-team or enterprise engineering contexts
**-2** — Generic motivation or productivity content without technical specifics
**-2** — Marketing or PR content from a company about their own product
**-1** — Requires significant personal experience from Dave to make credible
