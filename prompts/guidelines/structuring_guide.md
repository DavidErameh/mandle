# Structuring Guide

This guide covers how to structure different content types from the inside out — the logic of what goes where and why.

---

## Single Post Structure

The anatomy of a high-performing single take:

```
[Line 1: The hook — the reason to keep reading]
[Line 2: The development — what makes it true or specific]
[Line 3 (optional): The landing — the implication or the turn]
```

The best single posts follow a 1-2-1 rhythm: one short setup, two lines of development, one landing. But this is a pattern to feel, not a formula to execute mechanically. Let the idea determine the structure.

**What single posts should never do:**
- List things (that's what threads are for)
- Transition with "In conclusion..." or "The lesson here is..."
- End with a question that's really just an engagement bait line ("Agree?")

---

## Thread Structure: The 3-Act Arc

Every thread follows a compressed version of a 3-act structure, regardless of length:

**Act 1 — Hook (Tweet 1):** Creates the tension or the promise. This tweet must stand alone. Test: if someone sees only this tweet, do they have a reason to click "Show thread"?

**Act 2 — Development (Tweets 2–N-1):** Each tweet advances the argument, story, or breakdown. Each tweet is a scene, not a transition. Cut any tweet that is only there to connect two other tweets.

**Act 3 — Landing (Last tweet):** Delivers the payoff. Not a summary of what came before — a crystallization of the whole idea into one final thing. Sometimes this is the implication. Sometimes it's the surprising conclusion. Sometimes it's a question that changes how the reader sees everything before it.

---

## Breakdown Thread Structure

For technical reverse-engineering or how-it-works content:

```
Tweet 1: [The promise: "Here's how [thing] actually works — thread"]
Tweet 2: [The surface level — what everyone thinks it is]
Tweet 3: [The first layer down — what's actually happening]
Tweet 4: [The mechanism — why it works this way]
Tweet 5: [The edge case or the failure mode — where the model breaks]
Tweet 6: [What this means for how you build with it]
Tweet 7 (optional): [The practical takeaway or soft CTA]
```

## Day Series Structure

```
Day [X] of [cracking challenge to goal].

[One honest sentence: where things stand right now]
[One specific thing that happened today — concrete detail, not vague]
[Optional: what's next — one line max]
```

The Day Series should feel like a field note, not a press release. If the day was frustrating, say that. If the progress was small, say that. The audience follows the series because it's honest, not because it's triumphant.

---

## Bold Take + Evidence Structure

```
Tweet 1: [The take — stated without hedging, as a clear position]
Tweet 2: [The case for the take — the most specific evidence you have]
Tweet 3: [The counterargument, acknowledged and then addressed — this shows intellectual honesty]
Tweet 4: [The landing — what this means practically for the reader]
```

This structure is more credible than a pure polemic because it shows Dave has thought about the opposing view. The audience trusts a thinker who can acknowledge the other side and still land the same conclusion.

---

## The Ratios That Work

From the brand strategy research:
- Bold opinion/take posts: 4–6 per day (quick, punchy, opinionated)
- Media teases / progress: 2–3 per day (visual-anchored, curiosity gap)
- Thread breakdowns: 1–2 per day (authority building, funnel content)
- Day series: 1 per day (anchor, habit building)

Content generated by Mandle should reflect this ratio. The generator should not default to threads for every piece — single takes and bold opinions are the majority of the output volume.
